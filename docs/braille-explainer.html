The Very Basics

  • dot
  • cell
  • dots 123
  • dots 456

Braille is made of dots. They are always the same size and depth. Braille dots are arranged into cells, an arrangement of two dots wide by three dots high. These patterns of raised dots form tactile shapes the blind can recognize. Specific dots are referred to by number. The three left dots are 1, 2 and 3, and the three rights dots are 4, 5, and 6. A letter d is called a "dot 1 4 5."

  • letter r
  • question mark
  • radical!

There are only so many combinations a 2x3 grid can create. Most often a cell represents a letters or punctuation marks, but often enough more than one cell is used to represent a character. Cells flow from left to right forming phrases and sentences, breaking to new lines top to bottom to fill pages of text, just like print.

Letter W

The letter W resembles a J, but adds the bottom right dot, instead of both bottom dots (like it's end-of-of-the-alphabet neighbors). It's weird that way.

Why is W out of place like this? Blame France; Braille was invented in French, which at the time hadn't adopted the letter. So while the blind still learn the alphabet song despite this inconsistency.

Knowing this alphabet is the key to unlocking foreign language braille, as all roman alphabet languages use the same pattern.

Numbers

Basic numbers and math are written by using the number symbol as a prefix. Texts with lots of math are usually written in a specialized braille code called Nemeth Braille.

Use the Number Sign and everything afterward becomes a number. Letters A through I are one through nine and J is a zero.

Formatting and styling

Capital and italic letters are created using special characters along with the letters described above.

Capitals

A capital sign signals that an uppercase character is next. Use it once for an initial cap, twice to capitalize the whole word. Three starts an uppercase passage, with two more ending it.
